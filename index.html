00 Human Capital Enterprise Operating System

The Marine Corps manages people, money, training, and operational demand through disconnected systems. There is no shared decision layer connecting them into capability.

HCEOS is a federated operating model that links demand, supply, production, resources, and governance — without replacing authoritative systems.

01 The Real Problem

Six failures, one root cause.

The Marine Corps does not have a manpower problem. It has a capability production problem across disconnected domains. Each domain is internally rational. The enterprise is not.

01 · Structure

Disconnected Domains

M&RA, P&R, TECOM, MCRC, PP&O, and CD&I operate against domain-specific data, processes, and timelines. None can answer enterprise-level questions without manual reconciliation.

02 · Demand

No Demand Signal Integration

PP&O operational demand and CD&I future requirements never meet supply, production, and resource constraints in a single, traceable signal.

03 · Workflow

Invisible Workflows

Boards, awards, assignments, and exception cases move through email, SharePoint, and personal tracking. There is no system view of decisions in flight or stalled.

04 · Risk

No Enterprise Risk Model

Risk is described, not computed. Manpower, training, and funding risk cannot be aggregated into a single decision-grade view of where capability is failing.

05 · Optimization

Local Optimization, Enterprise Failure

Each domain optimizes against its own metrics. The sum is misaligned: filled billets without trained Marines, funded programs without throughput, demand without supply.

06 · Sprawl

Redundant System Growth

Every command builds its own dashboard, ontology, and AI pilot. Each one is rational alone. Together they create permanent fragmentation and recurring sustainment debt.

This is not a tooling issue.   It is a system design failure. No platform purchase resolves it. No single-domain pilot demonstrates it.
02 Core Insight

The risk is not building this wrong. The risk is building it five times.

Each organization is solving its own problem. None are solving the system. Five parallel ontologies, five governance models, five Foundry instances, five sustainment tails — all delivering versions of the same conclusion two years late.

03 Enterprise Capability System

A closed loop, not a stack.

Capability is produced through a sequence: demand → supply → production → execution. Resources and governance overlay every stage. Execution feeds demand. Today, this loop is open.

Today, this loop is broken at every seam: demand is not standardized, supply is not visible against demand, production is not measured against either, and execution feedback is anecdotal. HCEOS does not replace the loop — it makes it observable, computable, and decision-grade.

04 Architecture

Three layers. Authoritative systems unchanged.

The architecture is deliberately minimal. Most enterprise efforts fail because they re-implement the transactional layer. HCEOS does not. It governs a thin backbone, federates domain ontologies, and isolates the decision layer.

Layer 1

Enterprise Backbone

Minimal, governed, shared. The only objects every domain must agree on.

Person Organization Requirement Capability Resource Time

Six core entities. No more. Adding to this list requires enterprise-level sign-off. This is the discipline that prevents ontology sprawl.

Layer 2

Domain Ontologies

Owned and extended by each domain. Linked to backbone, not merged into it.

M&RA P&R TECOM MCRC PP&O CD&I

Each domain owns its own model, terminology, and refinement cadence. Federation, not unification. Domain ontologies extend the backbone — they do not replace it.

Layer 3

Decision Layer

Where cross-domain questions become answers a GO can act on.

Risk Forecasting Workflow routing Tradeoff analysis

The decision layer composes signals across domain ontologies. It is the only layer that produces enterprise-level outputs. It does not replace authority. It informs it.

Constraint.   Authoritative systems remain unchanged. MCTFS, MCTIMS, and similar systems of record are read from, not replaced. HCEOS adds a decision layer above the transactional layer. Anything that requires re-platforming a system of record is out of scope.

06 MVP · 90 Days

Prove the architecture, not the enterprise.

Three use cases. One ontology. One application surface. At least one cross-domain linkage demonstrated. The 90-day objective is not coverage. It is proof that the federated model holds.

1. Executive Common Operating Picture

A single view that connects validated requirements, available manpower, training throughput, and known constraints. One page. Decision-grade. No reconciliation.

2. Board Readiness

Boards as a workflow object — inputs, status, decisions, downstream effects — visible across M&RA and PP&O. The first proof that workflows are observable.

3. Awards Workflow

A bounded, well-understood workflow with cross-organizational handoffs. Demonstrates routing, ownership, and cycle-time measurement against a real process.

Hard Constraints

  • One ontology — extended, not duplicated.
  • One application surface — no command-by-command UI.
  • At least one cross-domain linkage demonstrated end-to-end.
  • Authoritative systems untouched. Read-only integration.
  • DC,I sign-off on data, classification, and AI usage before build.
  • Defined success criteria signed by sponsors before kickoff.
  • Hard stop at 90 days. No extension without GO-level decision.
07 Information Governance Constraint

DC,I is not a stakeholder. It is the boundary.

HCEOS only exists within DC,I authority. Architecture decisions made without DC,I alignment do not survive accreditation, do not scale across the Service, and create the next set of shadow systems.

Constraint 01
Data access — what can be linked, by whom, under what authority.
Constraint 02
Classification — what crosses domains, and how separation is enforced.
Constraint 03
AI policy — what the decision layer is permitted to compute, recommend, or automate.
Constraint 04
Accreditation — ATO path, environment of record, sustainment authority.
Constraint 05
Environment — alignment to ADVANA / WDP, including data residency and tooling.
If this does not align with DC,I, it does not scale.

Every architectural choice — backbone scope, ontology extension, AI capability, environment of record — is a DC,I decision before it is a domain decision.
08 Definition

What this is. What it is not.

Most failures in this space come from scope ambiguity. The lines below are not negotiable inside HCEOS.

HCEOS Is

  • A federated decision layer above authoritative systems.
  • A cross-domain integration model with an enforced backbone.
  • A workflow visibility and routing system for enterprise processes.
  • A governance frame for ontology growth across the Service.
  • A traceable basis for GO-level decisions and PPBE defense.

HCEOS Is Not

  • A replacement for MCTFS, MCTIMS, or any system of record.
  • A new system of record for any domain.
  • An AI decision-maker. AI informs; commanders decide.
  • An M&RA-only tool. It fails the moment it is scoped that way.
  • A platform purchase. The product is the operating model.
09 Where This Will Break

Be honest about how this fails.

These are not hypothetical. Each has occurred in comparable enterprise efforts. Naming them is the precondition for avoiding them.

No shared ownership of architecture

If no single body owns the backbone and ontology governance, every domain rebuilds. The architecture is the first thing to fund and the first thing to staff.

DC,I data restrictions

Cross-domain linkage is a data authority question before it is a tooling question. Without explicit DC,I direction, integration stalls at staff-level coordination.

P&R builds a parallel system

If P&R is treated as a consumer, not a co-architect, it builds a separate financial decision system. The Service then has two enterprise layers and zero alignment.

TECOM data not decision-grade

Training pipeline data is fragmented across MCTIMS, school-level systems, and spreadsheets. Without remediation, throughput cannot be modeled and capability gaps cannot be sourced to constraints.

PP&O demand signal unclear

If the operational requirement is not expressed as a structured, time-phased object, supply, production, and resources have nothing to align to.

M&RA workflows not standardized

Boards, assignments, awards, and exceptions vary by organization and personality. Workflow visibility cannot be built on processes that are not yet processes.

Ontology governance fails

Without a hard governance model, every domain extends the backbone unilaterally. Within 18 months the architecture is unrecognizable and unmaintainable.

Palantir creates a shadow system

If the integrator becomes the operator, the Service loses ownership of the model, the data, and the decisions. Vendor leverage compounds across every renewal.

Scope creep kills the MVP

Every stakeholder will ask for a tenth use case before the first three are stable. Without a sponsor willing to refuse, the 90-day MVP becomes an 18-month program with no decision output.

11 Implementation Approach

Six phases. Hard gates between each.

Each phase ends with a go / no-go gate. No phase advances on momentum. The decision is not "should we keep going?" — it is "have we cleared the gate?"

Phase 1

Enterprise Alignment

Sponsors, scope, ownership, and decision rights set across M&RA, P&R, TECOM, MCRC, PP&O, CD&I, DC,I.

Phase 2

Data Feasibility

What data exists, where, in what condition, and under what authority. DC,I-led.

Phase 3

Ontology Design

Backbone fixed. Domain ontologies extend, not duplicate. Governance model signed.

Phase 4

MVP Build

Three use cases. One surface. One ontology. Cross-domain linkage demonstrated.

Phase 5

Executive Decision

Service leadership reviews evidence — does the architecture hold, or does it not?

Phase 6

Scale or Stop

Either fund Service-wide federation under shared ownership, or terminate cleanly.

Hard gates.   Phase 1 → 2: documented sponsor commitment from every domain or no advance. Phase 2 → 3: DC,I authority to integrate the named data or no advance. Phase 3 → 4: signed ontology governance or no advance. Phase 4 → 5: at least one cross-domain decision produced end-to-end or no advance. Phase 5 → 6: GO-level decision recorded, with a defensible scale plan or a clean termination.
12 Closing

We do not need another system. We need a governed way to understand how the Marine Corps produces capability.

If this is built independently across organizations, it will fail before it scales.
